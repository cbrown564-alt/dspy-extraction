Thank you for asking me to review your patient, whom I saw in the neurology clinic today. He is a newly retired teacher who lives alone, maintains a regular daily routine, and reports reduced social contact since retirement. He attended unaccompanied and was able to give a clear account of his history.

He describes two distinct seizure types consistent with combined generalised and focal epilepsy. The focal events begin with a rising epigastric sensation and a brief pause with loss of awareness, during which he is noted to stare and fumble with nearby objects for up to 60–90 seconds, followed by several minutes of confusion. Separately, he has generalised tonic–clonic seizures that typically occur from sleep or in the early morning without a clear prodrome. There is no consistent trigger identified, though he reports that missed meals and fragmented sleep have occasionally preceded events.

Following introduction of lamotrigine, there has been a clear improvement in control, with just nine generalised tonic-clonic seizures documented this year to date. He also reports a reduction in the frequency of focal impaired-awareness episodes, now occurring roughly once every three to four weeks, usually clustering after periods of stress or disrupted sleep. He keeps a written seizure diary, which correlates with this history.

There is no history of status epilepticus. Post-ictally he experiences myalgia and headache for several hours, but there is no prolonged confusion beyond the immediate recovery period. He denies tongue-biting outside of the generalised events and has had two minor falls this year without injury. There is no current use of alcohol or recreational drugs, and he remains independent with activities of daily living. He has limited social contact but is open to community support should it be available.

Current anti-seizure medication is lamotrigine 150 mg twice daily, well tolerated without rash, dizziness, or diplopia. He is not on any other anti-seizure therapy at present, and there have been no recent medication changes. He is adherent and uses a pill organiser. Sleep hygiene is reasonable; he typically retires at 22:30 and rises at 06:30.

On examination today he was alert and oriented with normal language, intact cranial nerves, normal tone and power throughout, symmetrical reflexes, and a normal gait. There were no focal neurological deficits detected. Cardiovascular and general examination were unremarkable.

In summary, the clinical picture is of combined generalised and focal epilepsy, with both focal impaired-awareness seizures and separate generalised tonic–clonic seizures. Seizure control has improved on lamotrigine, with a clear reduction in generalised events this year as above. We discussed ongoing risk reduction, including consistent sleep routine, regular meals, and maintaining the seizure diary. He is not currently driving and understands DVLA guidance as applicable. I have provided information on local peer support groups to address social isolation, which he welcomed.

Plan:
- Continue lamotrigine 150 mg twice daily; no immediate dose change given current benefit and tolerability.
- Arrange routine EEG and updated MRI brain (epilepsy protocol) to further characterise both generalised and focal features.
- Check lamotrigine trough level, FBC, U&Es, LFTs, and vitamin D at next phlebotomy.
- Reinforce seizure diary and safety advice at home; provide written advice on when to seek urgent care.
- Follow-up in clinic in 6 months, or sooner should there be any deterioration or clustering of seizures.
