Thank you for asking me to review this patient in our epilepsy clinic. He attended with his partner, who provided collateral history. He works in a cleanroom/data-hall environment with strict access controls. He and his partner report that retrieval of rescue medication and obtaining refills is often significantly delayed while on site, which has led to more frequent missed doses. They both feel his seizure control has noticeably worsened over recent months in this context.

He has a history consistent with focal epilepsy. The typical events begin with a brief epigastric rising sensation and déjà vu, followed by behavioural arrest with rightward head version and non-purposeful picking movements of the left hand, lasting 60–90 seconds, with post-ictal confusion for up to 20 minutes. Secondary generalisation is rare but has occurred twice this year, both during early shifts when access to his medication locker was delayed. Weekly morning clusters reported; number per cluster not documented. Clusters are most often reported on days when doses are delayed at the start of his shift.

He is currently prescribed lamotrigine and lacosamide. He admits to missed or delayed morning doses on high-security access days, and to difficulty accessing buccal midazolam when a cluster begins. There is no recent head injury, intercurrent infection, or sleep deprivation beyond early shift starts. Alcohol intake is minimal. No new focal neurological symptoms between events.

Examination today was unremarkable. Routine bloods last month were within reference ranges. A prior MRI (2019) reported left mesial temporal sclerosis; interictal EEG (2020) showed left temporal sharp waves. Taken together with his semiology, this remains consistent with focal epilepsy of left temporal onset.

In summary, he has focal epilepsy with deterioration in seizure control strongly associated with delayed access to both regular and rescue medication in his workplace. Weekly morning clusters reported; number per cluster not documented. This pattern represents a clear decline from his prior baseline.

Plan:
- Optimise adherence: we discussed strategies to avoid delayed dosing at shift start, including pre-shift dosing and carrying a day-dose in a tamper-evident, security-approved container, as per employer policy (patient to confirm feasibility with occupational health).
- Rescue plan: reinforced use of buccal midazolam at onset of a second event within a cluster as previously instructed; patient to explore on-person carriage within workplace rules.
- Safety: advised temporary suspension of lone working and work at heights pending improved control (patient to liaise with line manager and occupational health).
- Investigations: arrange updated EEG and drug levels to exclude subtherapeutic dosing; consider ambulatory EEG if events remain frequent.
- Treatment: if clusters persist despite adherence measures, we will consider adding brivaracetam or perampanel; patient agrees in principle.
- Follow-up: 6 weeks, sooner if further generalised seizures or injuries occur.

Thank you for your ongoing care. Please let me know if there are workplace policy documents we should review jointly to facilitate safer access to his medications on site.
